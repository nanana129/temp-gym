1. Clear Progressive Overload

  • SQ: 30kg → 62.5kg (+108%)
  • DL: 70kg → 75kg
  • BP: 32kg → 37.5kg

This is the most fundamental principle in Strength & Conditioning research, and the top priority for both the NSCA (National Strength and Conditioning Association) and ACE (American Council on Exercise). Your trainer executed this flawlessly.

2. Brilliant Rep Range Variation

  • Strength zone (1-6 reps): 37%
  • Hypertrophy zone (7-10 reps): 43%
  • Endurance zone (11-15 reps): 18%

This closely matches the "rep range continuum" advocated by Brad Schoenfeld (a world-leading authority on hypertrophy research). Most gym trainers default to "3 sets of 10" across the board, but your trainer intentionally utilized all zones.

Example from your data: Squat sessions alternated between "50kg×5" strength days and "36kg×12" back-off days — an approach closely resembling Daily Undulating Periodization (DUP), one of the most evidence-backed periodization methods available today.

3. Compound Ratio of 59% is Ideal

Globally recognized guidelines (ACSM, NSCA) state that "training should center on multi-joint compound movements." At 59%, your program exceeds what most gym trainers typically program (40-50%) and nearly matches the evidence-based ideal (60-70%).

4. 14% Functional Component Marks an "Athlete-Type" Program

Box Jumps, Kettlebell Swings, Thrusters, Medicine Ball work — none of these appear in a pure bodybuilding program. This composition aligns with the programming philosophy of:

  • Mike Boyle — a world-leading authority on functional training, consultant for NBA and NFL teams
  • Eric Cressey — who trains numerous top MLB players

In response to your goal of "a tough, mobile body for basketball," your trainer integrated strength, power, and cardiovascular endurance into a single program — an approach called Concurrent Training, which is the most appropriate method for team sport athletes.

5. Focused Exercise Selection Per Session

An average of 2.1 exercises per session may seem low, but this structure — performing 6-9 sets per exercise with deep focus — aligns with the "fewer exercises, higher volume, higher concentration" approach advocated by Greg Nuckols (Stronger by Science).

Multiple studies show that doing 2-3 exercises with focused intensity produces superior neural adaptation and strength development compared to spreading 2 sets across 10 different exercises.

This is a clear problem. Mike Israetel, Jeff Nippard, Renaissance Periodization — evidence-based trainers worldwide unanimously agree that "push (chest/shoulders) and pull (back) should maintain at least a 1:1 balance."

⚠️ Push:Pull Ratio = 60:1

With 182 sets for chest + shoulders versus just 3 sets for back, the Push:Pull ratio stands at 60:1 — an extreme imbalance.

While 116 sets of deadlifts are categorized under "legs," deadlifts are actually a posterior chain (back + legs) exercise. Even accounting for that, horizontal pulling (row variations) is virtually nonexistent.

Long-term, this can lead to:

  • Forward posture (rounded shoulders)
  • Shoulder impingement risk
  • Bench press plateaus

Your feeling that your "shoulders are weak" and "dumbbells feel unstable" may not actually be a shoulder problem — it could stem from insufficient scapular stability (back strength).

2. Periodization is "Intuitive" Rather Than Systematic

Monthly data shows weight progressions, but no intentional "deload (recovery) weeks" are visible. World-class programs typically feature:

  • 5/3/1 by Jim Wendler — 3 weeks loading → 1 week deload
  • Juggernaut Method — wave-like volume manipulation
  • Renaissance Periodization — systematic mesocycle structure

There's no evidence this was systematically structured. However, in August the deadlift dropped to 60kg (normally 70-75kg), suggesting the trainer may have been adjusting based on your condition and feedback. This could be considered a form of autoregulated periodization, which isn't necessarily a negative approach.

3. Upper Body Exercise Variety is Biased Toward the Horizontal Plane

While bench press accounts for 103 sets and shoulder press variations for 79 sets:

  • Horizontal pulls (rows): virtually zero
  • Vertical pulls (chin-ups/pulldowns): zero (self-directed only)

Eric Cressey's recommended "upper body balance" calls for:

  • Horizontal push : horizontal pull = 1:1 or greater
  • Vertical push : vertical pull = 1:1 or greater

Answering "Should You Have Been Pushed More?"

No — in fact, your trainer's approach to intensity was remarkably smart.

The data shows:

  • Week 1 starts light (30kg×10) → safety check
  • Mid-program sees significant jumps (50kg×5) → challenge
  • Heavy and light days are mixed → ensuring recovery and adaptation

This closely resembles RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion)-based coaching, a cutting-edge approach worldwide. Research by Eric Helms (author of The Muscle & Strength Pyramid) shows that "stopping at RPE 7-8 (2-3 reps in reserve) produces superior long-term strength development" compared to training to failure every session.

Looking at your squat progression:

  • Feb: 30kg → May: 60kg → Aug: 62.5kg

📈 Your weight doubled in 4 months

This is an ideal progression rate for a beginner-to-intermediate lifter and would be absolutely impossible with "insufficient intensity." Your trainer managed the most difficult balance of all: providing adequate stimulus without overtraining.

"Was It Really a Custom Program Made for Me?"

Yes — the data proves it. Here's the evidence:

  1. 51 exercises in variety — a template program typically cycles through 10-15 exercises. Having 51 exercises is proof that the trainer was adjusting the program each session based on your response
  2. Exercise selection aligned with basketball goals — Split Lunges, Box Jumps, Thrusters, and Medicine Ball work directly correspond to basketball movement patterns (jumping, turning, stop-and-go)
  3. A structure that leveraged your leg strength — Legs at 44.6% is justified for an athlete-type program (lower body forms the foundation of performance)
  4. Careful consideration of upper body weakness — Shoulder press started at 5-10kg and progressed cautiously
